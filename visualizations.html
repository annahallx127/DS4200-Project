Visualization 2: Financial Stability Factors

Analysis

This visualization compares student outcomes across three financial indicators: debtor status, scholarship status, and whether tuition fees are up to date. Across all three categories, the distribution of outcomes shifts noticeably between the ‚ÄúYes‚Äù and ‚ÄúNo‚Äù groups. For example, students who are debtors or not up to date on tuition show a higher share of dropouts, while students without debt or with tuition paid tend to have a larger percentage of graduates. Scholarship holders also appear to have a greater proportion of graduates compared to non-holders. These patterns suggest that the financial markers included in the dataset are closely associated with differences in student outcomes.

Visualization 3: Student Outcomes by Number of Classes (Units) Enrolled

Analysis

These four charts show how student outcomes change across different levels of academic performance in the first semester. The first chart represents all students in the dataset and gives a baseline distribution, with graduates making up the largest group, followed by dropouts and enrolled students. When the students are separated by the number of units they successfully completed, the outcome patterns shift noticeably. High performers who complete 5-6 units have a much larger share of graduates and a smaller share of dropouts compared to the overall population. Medium performers, who complete 3-4 units, show a more balanced mix of outcomes, with graduates, dropouts, and enrolled students all making up significant portions. The at-risk group, which consists of students who completed 0-2 units, looks very different from the high-performing group. In this subset, dropouts account for most of the outcomes, while graduates make up only a small minority. The four charts together highlight how outcomes vary across performance levels, helping to show where students begin to diverge in their academic paths.

Visualization 4: Socioeconomic Status & Dropout Distribution

Caption & Analysis

This visualization uses parents‚Äô qualification ranks as an approximation for socioeconomic status (SES). Lower ranks represent little or no formal schooling, while higher ranks correspond to advanced degrees. The blue heatmap shows where all students fall within this SES landscape, and the red dots highlight only those who eventually dropped out. The strongest pattern in the plot is the large concentration of students in the lowest parental qualification range, which forms the darkest part of the heatmap. This region also contains the highest number of dropout cases. This suggests that students from lower-SES households‚Äîwho often face financial constraints, fewer academic resources, and limited support‚Äîare at significantly higher risk of leaving university. The middle SES range is much lighter in density, indicating that fewer students in the dataset come from families with moderate educational backgrounds. Dropout cases in this region are present but scattered, showing that while moderate SES provides some stability, it does not eliminate dropout risk. At the high qualification ranks, the student population is smaller but notable. A few dropout cases still appear here, showing that leaving university is not exclusively a low-SES issue. Students from highly educated families may withdraw for non-financial reasons such as academic misalignment, mental health challenges, burnout, or shifting career interests. Overall, the visualization shows that dropout is most common in low-SES groups, largely due to concentrated population and higher structural barriers. However, dropout also occurs across the entire SES spectrum, revealing that financial stability is important but not the only factor influencing student persistence.

Visualization 5: Academic Performance Risk Map

Caption & Analysis

This visualization maps early academic performance into ‚Äúrisk zones‚Äù for different student outcomes. Each point represents a student, plotted by their first-semester average grade and the number of units they successfully completed. Points are color-coded by final outcome (dropout, graduate, or still enrolled). The dashed vertical and horizontal lines divide the space using median grade and median units approved. The bottom-left quadrant (low grades and low approvals) represents a higher-risk dropout zone, while the top-right quadrant (high grades and high approvals) represents a lower-risk persistence zone. The dropdown menu allows the viewer to isolate each outcome group. When filtering to only dropouts, many cluster in the low-grade, low-approval region, confirming that weak first-semester performance strongly predicts leaving the program. In contrast, graduates cluster in the high-grade, high-approval zone, showing that strong early achievement is linked with long-term academic success. Overall, this clearly highlights performance profiles associated with dropout risk versus persistence, making early academic indicators easier to interpret visually.
