Financial Stability and Student Outcome

Caption & Analysis

This sunburst diagram demonstrates how financial stress factors, such as debtor status (has debt vs. no debt), tuition status (fees up to date vs. not up to date), and scholarship status (has scholarship vs. no scholarship) impacts the final academic outcome of students in higher education. This diagram highlights that students who do experience financial strain with debt and unpaid tuition are more likely to drop out compared to students without debt and their fees up to date. There are higher graduation rates amongst students that hold scholarships and have no debt. However, this diagram also shows how even among students in debt, those students with scholarships tend to have higher graduation outcomes than students without scholarships, indicating the meaningful role scholarships play in keeping students in school. Overall, this visualization reveals that financial and academic academic outcomes are deeply interconnected and financial support can have a signficant impact on student success.

Financial Stability Factors

Analysis

This visualization compares student outcomes across three financial indicators: debtor status, scholarship status, and whether tuition fees are up to date. Across all three categories, the distribution of outcomes shifts noticeably between the “Yes” and “No” groups. For example, students who are debtors or not up to date on tuition show a higher share of dropouts, while students without debt or with tuition paid tend to have a larger percentage of graduates. Scholarship holders also appear to have a greater proportion of graduates compared to non-holders. These patterns suggest that the financial markers included in the dataset are closely associated with differences in student outcomes.

Socioeconomic Status & Dropout Distribution

Caption & Analysis

This visualization uses parents' qualification ranks as an approximation for socioeconomic status (SES). Lower ranks represent little or no formal schooling, while higher ranks correspond to advanced degrees. The blue heatmap shows where all students fall within this SES landscape, and the red dots highlight only those who eventually dropped out. The strongest pattern in the plot is the large concentration of students in the lowest parental qualification range, which forms the darkest part of the heatmap. This region also contains the highest number of dropout cases. This suggests that students from lower-SES households, who often face financial constraints, fewer academic resources, and limited support, are at significantly higher risk of leaving university. The middle SES range is much lighter in density, indicating that fewer students in the dataset come from families with moderate educational backgrounds. Dropout cases in this region are present but scattered, showing that while moderate SES provides some stability, it does not eliminate dropout risk. At the high qualification ranks, the student population is smaller but notable. A few dropout cases still appear here, showing that leaving university is not exclusively a low-SES issue. Students from highly educated families may withdraw for non-financial reasons such as academic misalignment, mental health challenges, burnout, or shifting career interests. Overall, the visualization shows that dropout is most common in low-SES groups, largely due to concentrated population and higher structural barriers. However, dropout also occurs across the entire SES spectrum, revealing that financial stability is important but not the only factor influencing student persistence.

Student Outcomes by Number of Classes (Units) Enrolled

Analysis

These four charts show how student outcomes change across different levels of academic performance in the first semester. The first chart represents all students in the dataset and gives a baseline distribution, with graduates making up the largest group, followed by dropouts and enrolled students. When the students are separated by the number of units they successfully completed, the outcome patterns shift noticeably. High performers who complete 5-6 units have a much larger share of graduates and a smaller share of dropouts compared to the overall population. Medium performers, who complete 3-4 units, show a more balanced mix of outcomes, with graduates, dropouts, and enrolled students all making up significant portions. The at-risk group, which consists of students who completed 0-2 units, looks very different from the high-performing group. In this subset, dropouts account for most of the outcomes, while graduates make up only a small minority. The four charts together highlight how outcomes vary across performance levels, helping to show where students begin to diverge in their academic paths.

Academic Performance Risk Map

Caption & Analysis

This visualization shows how early academic performance relates to student outcomes. Each point represents a student based on their first-semester average grade and the number of units they passed. Students are color coded by final outcome. The dashed vertical and horizontal lines mark the median grade and median approvals, forming four performance zones. The bottom left zone contains many dropouts, while the top right reflects stronger performance and a higher likelihood of persistence. The dropdown lets the viewer focus on each outcome group. The added regression lines help clarify these patterns. The line for graduates slopes higher, showing that their performance improves more as grades and approvals increase. The dropout line is flatter and lower, reflecting weaker academic gains. The line for enrolled students sits between the two, indicating mixed progress. Together, the clusters and trend lines suggest that dropouts tend to start and remain lower performing, enrolled students show moderate progression, and graduates display the strongest upward trajectory.

Student Outcomes Based on First Semester Performance

Caption & Analysis

This visualization demonstrates the relationship between first-semester academic performance and student outcomes, where the x-axis indicates the number of courses a student has completed and therefore received an evaluation for (meaning they took their exams and were eligible for review), and the y-axis indicates the number of courses the student successfully received a passing grade in. Each point represents one student, and the lines indicate the trends for each outcome group (graduate, enrolled, and dropout). Overall, a pattern showing that students who were evaluated in more courses and passed more of them in their first semester are far more likely to graduate is visible from this visualization. However, it also shows that students with very few or zero evaluations/approvals cluster heavily in the dropout group, suggesting there was early academic difficulty from the beginning of their journeys. Therefore, dropouts are concentrated near the origin of the plot, whereas graduates appear more consistently in the higher-performance regions. This reinforces the idea that early academic performance amplifies the existing inequalities and the issues that may impact a student's performance in the long run.

Key Findings & Summary

Primary Insight

The visualizations reveal consistent patterns linking financial circumstances, early academic performance, and family background with the three student outcomes in this dataset. While we cannot determine the exact reasons behind each student's outcome, the patterns highlight potential factors that appear closely associated with whether students graduate, stay enrolled, or drop out. These trends especially point to groups of students whose academic paths diverge early, often alongside financial strain or first-semester academic challenges.

Major Findings

  • Finding 1: Students with debt or unpaid tuition appear more frequently among dropouts, while financially stable students have a higher proportion of graduates.
    In the sunburst and bar charts, financial strain aligns with a higher share of dropout outcomes, suggesting money-related challenges may influence how students progress.
  • Finding 2: Scholarship holders tend to have stronger outcomes.
    Even within the groups experiencing debt or unpaid tuition, students with scholarships show a larger percentage of graduates compared to those without scholarship support.
  • Finding 3: First-semester academic performance aligns closely with final outcomes.
    Students who complete and pass more courses in their first semester cluster in the graduate group, while dropouts are concentrated among students with fewer evaluations and approvals.
  • Finding 4: Students whose parents have lower educational qualifications appear more frequently in the dropout group.
    The SES visualization shows higher dropout density among students whose parents have limited schooling, indicating that background factors may shape early academic experiences.
  • Finding 5: No single feature explains student outcomes alone.
    Financial factors, early performance, and socioeconomic background each show patterns across different visualizations, suggesting that multiple conditions interact throughout a students academic path.

Implications for Higher Education

These patterns suggest that financial, academic, and background-related factors all matter when thinking about student persistence. For universities, this means that identifying students who might need extra support requires looking beyond just grades or financial records on their own. Paying closer attention to these patterns earlier, especially during the transition into the first semester, may help institutions offer more timely support through advising, academic resources, or financial guidance.

Recommendations

  • Expand financial guidance and emergency aid options for students who begin their program with debt or unpaid tuition.
  • Strengthen early-semester academic support through tutoring, study workshops, or structured advising for students struggling in their first term.
  • Create targeted mentorship or transition programs for students from families with lower educational backgrounds to support their adjustment to university life.

Limitations & Future Work

This dataset comes from a single institution and does not capture factors like mental health, work responsibilities, or personal circumstances that can also influence academic outcomes. Future work could include additional qualitative or behavioral information, or expand the analysis to multiple universities to see whether these patterns remain consistent in broader contexts.
