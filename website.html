Project Introduction

Student dropout and academic success have become major concerns for higher education institutions as they directly impact graduation rates, institutional reputation, and student well-being. Understanding the factors that contribute to whether a student completes a degree or leaves early is essential for improving retention strategies and educational quality.

The Predict Students Dropout and Academic Success dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository provides extensive information on student demographics, financial circumstances, academic performance, and social engagement, offering a valuable opportunity to examine the relationships between these variables and student outcomes.

Through the use of data visualization, meaningful patterns and trends can be uncovered to reveal how elements such as family support, attendance, grades, and previous education influence academic trajectories. Analyzing these insights through visual exploration makes the data more interpretable for educators and administrators, allowing for data-driven decisions that foster student success and reduce dropout rates.

This topic is both important and interesting because it bridges data science with educational research, transforming raw institutional data into actionable knowledge that can support long-term improvements in higher education.

Data Introduction

The dataset we are using for this project is called “Predict Students Dropout and Academic Success,” and it comes from the UCI Machine Learning Repository, which is a public online source for research data. It was created in 2021 by researchers at the Center for Machine Learning and includes information on 4,424 students enrolled in different undergraduate programs, such as agronomy, design, education, nursing, journalism, management, social service, and technologies.

The dataset has 36 features that cover details known at the time of enrollment, like demographics, family background, financial information, and previous academic experience. It also includes performance data from the first and second semesters. The data is organized in a table format and contains a mix of numbers and categories, and it has already been cleaned to remove missing values and unusual data points.

The main goal of the dataset is to classify students into three groups: dropout, enrolled, or graduate. Because one of these outcomes is much more common than the others, the data is imbalanced, which is important to keep in mind when analyzing it. This dataset is useful because it brings together many different factors that can affect a student’s academic path and gives us the chance to study how these factors relate to success or dropout in higher education.
